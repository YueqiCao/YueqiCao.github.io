The Linear Regression Model

A normal linear regression model assumes an output variable YYY, a vector of covariates x=(x1,⋯ ,xp){\bf x}=(x_1,\cdots ,x_p)x=(x1​,⋯,xp​), and YYY is linear deterministic function βTx\bf \beta^TxβTx with additive Gaussian noise. That is

Y=βTx+ϵ,ϵ∼N(0,σ2).Y={\bf \beta^Tx}+\epsilon, \epsilon\sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma^2). Y=βTx+ϵ,ϵ∼N(0,σ2).

Given nnn i.i.d. samples (x1,y1),⋯ ,(xn,yn)({\bf x_1},y_1),\cdots, ({\bf x_n},y_n)(x1​,y1​),⋯,(xn​,yn​), the joint probability density of observed data y1,⋯ ,yny_1,\cdots, y_ny1​,⋯,yn​ conditional upon x1,⋯ ,xn\bf x_1,\cdots,x_nx1​,⋯,xn​ and the value of β\bf \betaβ and σ2\sigma^2σ2 (or, the likelihood function) is

p(y1,⋯ ,yn∣x1,⋯ ,xn,β,σ2)=∏i=1np(yi∣xi,β,σ2)=(2πσ2)−n/2exp⁡{−12σ2∑i=1n(yi−βTxi)2}.\begin{aligned} &p(y_1,\cdots,y_n|{\bf x_1,\cdots,x_n},\beta,\sigma^2)=\prod_{i=1}^np(y_i|{\bf x_i},\beta,\sigma^2)\\ &=(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2}\exp\{-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\beta^T{\bf x_i})^2\}. \end{aligned} ​p(y1​,⋯,yn​∣x1​,⋯,xn​,β,σ2)=i=1∏n​p(yi​∣xi​,β,σ2)=(2πσ2)−n/2exp{−2σ21​i=1∑n​(yi​−βTxi​)2}.​

Maximizing the likelihood function is equivalent to minimizing the sum of squared residuals SSR(β)=∑i=1n(yi−βTxi)2SSR(\beta)=\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\beta^T{\bf x_i})^2SSR(β)=∑i=1n​(yi​−βTxi​)2. Let X=[x1,⋯ ,xn]T\bf X=[\bf x_1,\cdots,x_n]^TX=[x1​,⋯,xn​]T and y=(y1,⋯ ,yn)T{\bf y}=(y_1,\cdots, y_n)^Ty=(y1​,⋯,yn​)T. We have

SSR(β)=(y−Xβ)T(y−Xβ)=∥y−Xβ∥22.SSR(\beta)=(\bf y-X\beta)^T(y-X\beta)=\|y-X\beta\|_2^2. SSR(β)=(y−Xβ)T(y−Xβ)=∥y−Xβ∥22​.

The minimizer, also called the ordinary least square (OLS) estimate, is given in the closed form (suppose XTX\bf X^TXXTX is invertible)

β^ols=(XTX)−1XTy.\hat{\beta}_{ols}=\bf (X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty. β^​ols​=(XTX)−1XTy.

The closed form can be obtained using linear algebra, which is often explained as the geometric meaning of least square.

Bayesian Linear Regression

A Semiconjugate Prior for β\betaβ

Suppose β∼N(β0,Σ0)\beta\sim\mathcal N(\beta_0,\Sigma_0)β∼N(β0​,Σ0​). The posterior distribution for β\betaβ is

p(β∣y,X,σ2)∝p(y∣X,β,σ2)p(β)∝exp⁡{−12(−2βTXTy/σ2+βTXTXβ/σ2−2βTΣ0−1β0+βTΣ0−1β)}=exp⁡{βT(Σ0−1β0+XTy/σ2)−12βT(Σ0−1+XTX/σ2)β}.\begin{aligned} &p(\beta|{\bf y,X,\sigma^2})\propto p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\beta,\sigma^2})p(\beta)\\ \propto& \exp\{\bf -\frac{1}{2}(-2\beta^TX^Ty/\sigma^2+\beta^TX^TX\beta/\sigma^2-2\beta^T\Sigma_0^{-1}\beta_0+\beta^T\Sigma_0^{-1}\beta)\}\\ =&\exp\{\bf \beta^T(\Sigma_0^{-1}\beta_0+X^Ty/\sigma^2)-\frac{1}{2}\beta^T(\Sigma_0^{-1}+X^TX/\sigma^2)\beta\}. \end{aligned} ∝=​p(β∣y,X,σ2)∝p(y∣X,β,σ2)p(β)exp{−21​(−2βTXTy/σ2+βTXTXβ/σ2−2βTΣ0−1​β0​+βTΣ0−1​β)}exp{βT(Σ0−1​β0​+XTy/σ2)−21​βT(Σ0−1​+XTX/σ2)β}.​

Therefore, the posterior distribution for β\betaβ is Gaussian with

E[β∣y,X,σ2]=(Σ0−1+XTX/σ2)−1(Σ0−1β0+XTy/σ2)Var[β∣y,X,σ2]=(Σ0−1+XTX/σ2)−1.\begin{aligned} &\mathbb E[{\bf \beta|y,X,\sigma^2}]=(\Sigma_0^{-1}+{\bf X^TX/\sigma^2})^{-1}(\Sigma_0^{-1}\beta_0+{\bf X^Ty/\sigma^2})\\ &\textrm{Var}[{\bf \beta|y,X,\sigma^2}]=(\Sigma_0^{-1}+{\bf X^TX/\sigma^2})^{-1}. \end{aligned} ​E[β∣y,X,σ2]=(Σ0−1​+XTX/σ2)−1(Σ0−1​β0​+XTy/σ2)Var[β∣y,X,σ2]=(Σ0−1​+XTX/σ2)−1.​

Suppose β0=0\beta_0=0β0​=0 and Σ0=α−1I\Sigma_0=\alpha^{-1}{\bf I}Σ0​=α−1I. The posterior density is simplified as

p(β∣y,X,σ2)∝exp⁡{−12(βT(αI+XTX/σ2)β−2βTXTy/σ2)}.p(\beta|{\bf y,X,\sigma^2})\propto \exp\{-\frac{1}{2}(\beta^T({\bf \alpha I+X^TX/\sigma^2})\beta-2\beta^T{\bf X^Ty}/\sigma^2)\}. p(β∣y,X,σ2)∝exp{−21​(βT(αI+XTX/σ2)β−2βTXTy/σ2)}.

Maximizing the posterior probability is equivalent to minimizing the error

1σ2∑i=1n(yi−βTxi)2+αβTβ,\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\beta^T{\bf x_i})^2+\alpha\beta^T\beta, σ21​i=1∑n​(yi​−βTxi​)2+αβTβ,

which leads to the regularized least square problem.

A Semiconjugate Prior for σ2\sigma^2σ2

Let γ=1σ2\gamma=\frac{1}{\sigma^2}γ=σ21​ be the precision. Suppose γ∼Gamma(ν0/2,νoσ02/2)\gamma\sim Gamma(\nu_0/2,\nu_o\sigma^2_0/2)γ∼Gamma(ν0​/2,νo​σ02​/2). The posterior probability for γ\gammaγ is

p(γ∣y,X,β)∝p(γ)p(y∣X,β,γ)∝(γν0/2−1exp⁡{−γν0σ0/2})×(γn/2exp⁡{−γSSR(β)/2})=γ(ν0+n)/2−1exp⁡{−γ(ν0σ02+SSR(β))/2}.\begin{aligned} &p(\gamma|{\bf y,X,\beta})\propto p(\gamma)p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\beta,\gamma})\\ \propto&(\gamma^{\nu_0/2-1}\exp\{-\gamma\nu_0\sigma^0/2\})\times(\gamma^{n/2}\exp\{-\gamma SSR(\beta)/2\})\\ =&\gamma^{(\nu_0+n)/2-1}\exp\{-\gamma(\nu_0\sigma^2_0+SSR(\beta))/2\}. \end{aligned} ∝=​p(γ∣y,X,β)∝p(γ)p(y∣X,β,γ)(γν0​/2−1exp{−γν0​σ0/2})×(γn/2exp{−γSSR(β)/2})γ(ν0​+n)/2−1exp{−γ(ν0​σ02​+SSR(β))/2}.​

Hence, the posterior distribution for γ\gammaγ is Gamma((ν0+n)/2,(ν0σ02+SSR(β))/2)Gamma((\nu_0+n)/2,(\nu_0\sigma_0^2+SSR(\beta))/2)Gamma((ν0​+n)/2,(ν0​σ02​+SSR(β))/2).

Gibbs Sampler

Given current values {β(s),(σ2)(s)}\{\beta^{(s)},(\sigma^2)^{(s)}\}{β(s),(σ2)(s)}. We can sample a new value by

  1. Updating β\betaβ:
    1. Compute E[β∣y,X,(σ2)(s)]\mathbb E[\beta|{\bf y,X,(\sigma^2)^{(s)}}]E[β∣y,X,(σ2)(s)] and Var[β∣y,X,(σ2)(s)]\textrm{Var}[\beta|{\bf y,X,(\sigma^2)^{(s)}}]Var[β∣y,X,(σ2)(s)];
    2. Sample β(s+1)\beta^{(s+1)}β(s+1) according to N(E,Var)\mathcal N(\mathbb E,\textrm{Var})N(E,Var).
  2. Updating σ2\sigma^2σ2:
    1. Compute SSR(β(s+1))SSR(\beta^{(s+1)})SSR(β(s+1));
    2. Sample (σ2)(s+1)(\sigma^2)^{(s+1)}(σ2)(s+1) according to inverse-gamma.

Zellner’s g-Prior

In the setting of prior distribution of β\betaβ, let β0=0\beta_0=0β0​=0 and Σ0=k(XTX)−1\Sigma_0=k({\bf X^TX})^{-1}Σ0​=k(XTX)−1, where k=gσ2k=g\sigma^2k=gσ2. We obtain what is called g-prior. The mean and variance are simplified as

E[β∣y,X,σ2]=gg+1σ2ΦVar[β∣y,X,σ2]=gg+1ΦXTy.\begin{aligned} &\mathbb E[\beta|{\bf y,X,\sigma^2}]=\frac{g}{g+1}\sigma^2{\bf \Phi}\\ &\textrm{Var}[\beta|{\bf y,X,\sigma^2}]=\frac{g}{g+1}{\bf \Phi X^Ty}. \end{aligned} ​E[β∣y,X,σ2]=g+1g​σ2ΦVar[β∣y,X,σ2]=g+1g​ΦXTy.​

where Φ=(XTX)−1\bf\Phi=(X^TX)^{-1}Φ=(XTX)−1. Suppose the prior distribution for σ2\sigma^2σ2 is inverse-gamma. We will show that p(σ2∣y,X)p(\sigma^2|{\bf y,X})p(σ2∣y,X) is inverse-gamma. Compared with the above discussion, this simplifies our sampling procedure since the posterior does NOT depend on β\betaβ.

Note that

p(y∣X,σ2)=∫p(y∣X,σ2,β)p(β∣X,σ2)dβ.p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\sigma^2})=\int p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\sigma^2,\beta})p(\beta|{\bf X,\sigma^2}){\rm d}\beta. p(y∣X,σ2)=∫p(y∣X,σ2,β)p(β∣X,σ2)dβ.

Substituting the densities, we obtain

p(y∣X,σ2,β)p(β∣X,σ2)=Cexp⁡{−12σ2(y−Xβ)T(y−Xβ)−12gσ2βTΦ−1β}.\begin{aligned} p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\sigma^2,\beta})p(\beta|{\bf X,\sigma^2})&=C \exp\{-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}({\bf y-X\beta})^T({\bf y-X\beta})-\frac{1}{2g\sigma^2}\beta^T{\bf \Phi^{-1}}\beta\}. \end{aligned} p(y∣X,σ2,β)p(β∣X,σ2)​=Cexp{−2σ21​(y−Xβ)T(y−Xβ)−2gσ21​βTΦ−1β}.​

where

C=(2πσ2)−n/2×∣2πgσ2Φ∣−1/2.C=(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2}\times|2\pi g\sigma^2{\Phi}|^{-1/2}. C=(2πσ2)−n/2×∣2πgσ2Φ∣−1/2.

The terms in the exponent can be rewritten as

−12σ2yTy−12(β−m)TV−1(β−m)+12mTV−1m.-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}{\bf y^Ty}-\frac{1}{2}(\beta-m)^TV^{-1}(\beta-m)+\frac{1}{2}m^TV^{-1}m. −2σ21​yTy−21​(β−m)TV−1(β−m)+21​mTV−1m.

where

V=gg+1σ2Φm=gg+1ΦXTy.\begin{aligned} &V=\frac{g}{g+1}\sigma^2{\bf \Phi}\\ &m=\frac{g}{g+1}{\bf \Phi X^Ty}. \end{aligned} ​V=g+1g​σ2Φm=g+1g​ΦXTy.​

The integration is

p(y∣X,σ2)=(2π)−n/2(1+g)−p/2(σ2)−n/2exp⁡{−12σ2SSRg},p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\sigma^2})=(2\pi)^{-n/2}(1+g)^{-p/2}(\sigma^2)^{-n/2}\exp\{-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}SSR_g\}, p(y∣X,σ2)=(2π)−n/2(1+g)−p/2(σ2)−n/2exp{−2σ21​SSRg​},

where

SSRg=yT(I−gg+1XTΦX)y.SSR_g=\bf y^T(I-{\it \frac{g}{g+1}}X^T\Phi X)y. SSRg​=yT(I−g+1g​XTΦX)y.

Let γ=1/σ2∼Gamma(ν0/2,ν0σ02/2)\gamma=1/\sigma^2\sim Gamma(\nu_0/2,\nu_0\sigma^2_0/2)γ=1/σ2∼Gamma(ν0​/2,ν0​σ02​/2). Then we have

p(γ∣X,y)∝p(γ)p(y∣X,γ)∝γ(ν0+n)/2−1exp⁡{−γ(ν0σ02+SSRg)/2}.\begin{aligned} p(\gamma|{\bf X,y})&\propto p(\gamma)p({\bf y}|{\bf X,\gamma})\\ &\propto \gamma^{(\nu_0+n)/2-1}\exp\{-\gamma(\nu_0\sigma^2_0+SSR_g)/2\}. \end{aligned} p(γ∣X,y)​∝p(γ)p(y∣X,γ)∝γ(ν0​+n)/2−1exp{−γ(ν0​σ02​+SSRg​)/2}.​

Therefore, the posterior distribution for σ2\sigma^2σ2 is inverse-gamma with parameter ((ν0+n)/2,(ν0σ02+SSRg)/2)((\nu_0+n)/2,(\nu_0\sigma^2_0+SSR_g)/2)((ν0​+n)/2,(ν0​σ02​+SSRg​)/2).

Monte Carlo Sampler

Using g-prior, we see that the posterior distribution of σ2\sigma^2σ2 does not contain β\betaβ. Thus we can use a simple sampling procedure

  1. Sample σ2\sigma^2σ2 according to inverse-gamma;
  2. Sample β∼N(gg+1β^ols,gg+1σ2Φ)\beta\sim\mathcal N(\frac{g}{g+1}\hat{\beta}_{ols},\frac{g}{g+1}\sigma^2{\bf \Phi})β∼N(g+1g​β^​ols​,g+1g​σ2Φ).
